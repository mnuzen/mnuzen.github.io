The Traveler

“Let me tell you something,” she says

Her fingers reach out and

Tuck themselves into the spill of my sleeve.

Her mouth curls into a smile,

Wine-dark and secretive

In the near dark.

We are on the fresh grass of a dark lawn,

Underneath an open sky.

Neighborhood lights glint

Against her hair.

My ankle brushes a bare patch of earth.

We are everywhere,

All at once.

“I can travel,” she says.

“Anywhere I want. Whenever I want.”

“I don’t believe you,” is the first thing I want to say.

I don’t say it. I say,

“Show me.”

She pulls out a pair of

White headphones.

Plugs in the jack into her phone

In the near darkness

As though she’s done it a hundred thousand times.

Offers one earbud in a way that

I can’t refuse.

So I take the bud.

White light from her screen.

Her nails tap.

Music starts

Through our shared headphones:

the soft chords of a piano,

Notes strung together delicately,

Swaying, like ships at sea.

She says, “This is Italy.”

She says, “Can you hear the waterways?

“Can you hear how pretty it is?

“The swaying gondolas, and

“The cobblestone streets, and

“Stone footbridges and—”

And I recognize the song.

It’s something by Mendelssohn.

I catch a glimpse of the screen:

Venetian Gondola Song, Opus 30, Number 6.

“I think so,” I say.

“Close your eyes,” she says.
I close my eyes.

Sunlight hits the water just right,

Illuminates every ripple and lights up

The row of beautiful gondola boats

Swaying by the promenade.

An old cafe, on the other side of the street.

The smell of hot Italian espresso

Lingering in the air.

Purple wisteria flowers crawl

Over window flower boxes,

Spilling out of stone cracks.

An oil painting kind of scene

Like someone painted in busy bougainvillea

With paper-thin strokes of purple,

Dragged white across the blue sky in

Broad, sweeping strokes

To create clouds.

Timeless.

“I think—” I start to say.

I don’t finish.

I’m about to open my eyes.

“Don’t,” she says. I hear her tap her phone.

The song changes.

“This is Russia.”

She reads to me before I can ask,

“Stravinsky’s Firebird suite.”

An orchestral piece

Cellos calling out dark

Deep forests.

Violas playing a violet sky.

Scarlet feathers of a phoenix

Litter the floor like

Breadcrumbs in a fairytale.

There is something

Undeniably magical about it:

The smell of petrichor and

The leaves crumbling underfoot and

The untamed and

Untouched wilderness.

The song ends.

There’s a breath, a pause, before the next one begins.

“This is Austria.”

A small cottage somewhere in the countryside:

A small home with wildflowers in the front

And an enormous garden in the back.

Sunlight dappling my bare shoulders

As I walk

Under lemon trees.

I smell wild herbs and

Fipe tomatoes and

Yellow summer. Plump olives and

Unruly dandelions and the constant sound of bees—

Their smell, their feet

Brushed with the spices of a thousand different flowers.

Picking blood oranges and

Discovering mosquito-breeding ground by the rain barrel next to the shed,

Following the buzz of goldfuzzed bees; and

Walking, stumbling,

Half-drunk on the scent of pollen, the

Mellow dandelion wine swirling in the air.

Sun-broken fields, the land swallowed in summer.

Bare feet on raw earth.

The taste of sunlight.

The song ends. A pause.

“This is Korea,” she says.

I don’t recognize this song, but it’s

Something more upbeat, k-pop.

We spend a while in Seoul, a

Fast and glamorous city

Full of people young and hungry and chasing something.

Fish cakes and roasted sweet potatoes and

Lamb skewers and candied peanuts

In the night market.

Bumper-to-bumper traffic,

Red tail-lights burning into a deep red blur.

A pause.

Guitar, this time.

“This is Nicaragua.”

A bicycle food cart selling soft drinks and chicharrones.

Another selling wedges of mangos,

Soft and sweet and ripe and yellow.

“This is Canada.”

Mountain cliffs growing up and up and up

And scraping at an impossibly large sky.

This is wildflowers

Blooming at twilight,

And a hundred thousand diamonds

Scattered across the velvet sky.

This is open air and

Ancient rock and

History untouched.

This is Venice.

This is Carthage.

This is Tel Aviv and Daegu and

Hanoi and Managua and

Everywhere, all at once

All of the places you could ever want to go.

“Let me tell you something,” she says.

She smiles,

Wine-dark and far too knowing.

We’re sitting on a dark lawn,

But we’re everywhere,

All at once.

“I can travel to anywhere I want. Whenever I want.”

I watch as she rolls up

The white wires of her headphones,

Quick and efficient,

As though she’s done so a

Hundred thousand times. I say,

“I believe you.”

If Cassini Were a Person

Afterwards, this is the question she asks:

“When will we go again?”

She’s the kind of person you write about

The kind of person you see in a coffee shop and

Wish you could take a picture

She’s

Three black hair ties around her wrist

Nails short and efficient but

Painted sparkling silver

She’s

Good with a camera and even better with a pen

Quick, dry humor and an infectious laugh

At every moment

She’s

Got a little patch of freckles on her left elbow

A constellation that she swears is a map

Of the solar system

The kind of person who wants to figure out

How to bottle up the stars and pour them

Into her coffee

So she can taste a bit of the universe

Every morning

The kind of person who’s

A starry-eyed tourist in her own town

Peering at everything through fresh eyes

Her lipstick is peach pink and she wears

Those little gold earrings in the shapes of

Planets and stars and comets

She loves her mother

She loves her planet

She wonders where we are from

She takes you on an adventure

Asks, spontaneously, for a day out

A road trip

She’s

Driving, pushing 90 on the freeway with the windows down

The music turned up

A handheld camera shakily recording

Every moment

She’s

Jumping barefoot into river water and

Sunbathing on rocky shores

Watching the silver fish swim by and

Examining the rocks at the bottom of the whirlpool

She’s

The person you think of

When you see the sun-stained polaroids on your wall

When you see the silver riverstones collected on your windowsill

When you see the pages and pages

And pages and pages you wrote

In your journal

She’s

The person you learn from

Jumping in barefoot and driving too fast

Painting your nails in the colors of the solar system and staring starry-eyed

At things you’ve seen before

And recording every moment

And afterwards,

You ask,

“When will we go again?”
